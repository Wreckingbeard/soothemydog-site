Dogs, Work, and the Quiet House

Why so many dogs fall apart when everyone goes back to the office, and what actually helps.

During the stay-at-home years, a whole generation of dogs grew up with their people around almost all the time. Then the world went back to work, and for many of those dogs the house suddenly emptied out for eight or nine hours a day. They never had the chance to learn that being alone is safe.

This is real, and it is common

Even before the pandemic, veterinarians estimated that roughly one in five dogs experienced some degree of separation anxiety. As people returned to in-person work, veterinary professionals reported a noticeable rise in cases. The American Veterinary Medical Association has highlighted this “back-to-work” anxiety and urged owners to prepare their dogs for time apart.

It helps to remember what separation anxiety actually is. It is not spite, and it is not stubbornness. It is a real panic response. A dog barking, pacing, or chewing the door when left alone is frightened, not misbehaving. That difference matters, because it changes how you help them.

Where calming music fits

Gentle, continuous music can give an anxious dog a steady sound to focus on when the house goes quiet. It is not a cure on its own. But alongside the real work, short practice sessions at being alone, some enrichment, a comfortable spot, and your veterinarian's guidance, a lot of owners find it a simple, calming addition to the routine. Press play as you pick up your keys, and let it run while you are gone.

A note from us: Calming music can help many dogs relax, but it is not a substitute for veterinary care. If your dog shows signs of severe anxiety, distress, or a medical problem, please talk with your veterinarian about a plan that fits your dog.
