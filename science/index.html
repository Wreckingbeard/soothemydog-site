The Science of Calming Music for Dogs

What the peer-reviewed research actually says about music, dogs, and stress. In plain terms.

Music is not a cure for anxiety, and no song replaces training or veterinary care. But a growing body of peer-reviewed research suggests that calm, classical-style music can help many dogs relax in stressful settings. Here is what the studies actually found.

Calm music, calmer dogs

In one of the earliest controlled studies, Wells and colleagues (2002) played different types of auditory stimulation to dogs in a rescue shelter. Classical music was associated with more resting and quiet behavior. Other genres were not, and some, like heavy metal, were linked to more agitation.

Kogan and colleagues (2012) compared classical music, heavy metal, and music arranged for dogs. Dogs exposed to classical music spent more time sleeping and vocalized less, while heavy metal was linked to more body shaking and unrest.

Measurable signs of relaxation

Bowman and colleagues (2015, 2017) went further, measuring physiological stress in kenneled dogs. Classical music was associated with reduced signs of stress, including changes in heart-rate variability (a recognized marker of a calmer nervous system), alongside more lying down and less barking.

Why variety matters

The same research team noticed something useful: when the same music played day after day, its calming effect faded as dogs grew used to it. In other words, fresh and varied music tends to hold its effect better than a single track on repeat. That finding is exactly why we create new, varied sessions instead of looping one piece. We want the calm to keep working.

The honest summary

A 2020 review of this literature in the journal Animals concluded that auditory enrichment, and classical music in particular, can have a modest calming influence on dogs in stressful environments, while cautioning that effects vary between individual dogs and that music is one tool among many. That is our view too: helpful for many dogs, worth trying, and best used alongside good routines and your veterinarian's guidance.

A note from us: Calming music can help many dogs relax, but it is not a substitute for veterinary care. If your dog shows signs of severe anxiety, distress, or a medical problem, please talk with your veterinarian about a plan that fits your dog.
