How Our Music Is Made

The short version: slow, simple, piano-led music at a calm pace, kept gentle on the ears and free of sudden changes, with a fresh session every day.

People ask what really goes into calming music for a dog, so here is the plain version. Every Soothe My Dog video rests on the same few choices, and each one traces back to what the research on dogs and music actually supports. No mysticism, no magic frequencies. Just careful, deliberate music.

How is Soothe My Dog's music made?

We keep it slow and simple. A small set of calm, gentle piano notes carries the melody, with warm, soft sounds underneath. The pace sits at a slow, resting speed of about 56 beats per minute. We hold it to one lead instrument and a few soft layers so nothing feels busy, and we make a new session every day instead of replaying one track.

What tempo is calming for a dog?

Slow. Music built to calm dogs leans on a slow, resting tempo, and ours holds steady at about 56 beats per minute. An even, unhurried pace gives a dog nothing to startle at, which is the whole point when you are trying to help one settle.

Why a simple, piano-led arrangement?

A single lead instrument and a simple, gentle arrangement are the ideas behind music made to calm dogs, the approach popularized in the Through a Dog's Ear work of Joshua Leeds and Lisa Spector. We follow it: soft piano out front, a warm bed of sound behind it, and no clutter competing for a dog's attention.

Why does the music change every day?

Because sameness stops working. When researchers played the same music to kenneled dogs day after day, the calming effect faded as the dogs grew used to it, and varying the music helped it keep working (Bowman and colleagues, 2017). So we do not loop one track. Every day is a fresh, varied session.

Is it gentle on my dog's ears?

Yes, and we design for it. Dogs hear much higher pitches than we do, so we ease off the piercing high end and hold the whole mix at a steady, even level with no sudden jumps in volume. A sudden loud moment is exactly what wakes a resting dog, so we take those out.

What the research supports

Studies of shelter and kenneled dogs have found that calm, classical-style music is linked to less stress: more resting and lying down, less barking, and higher heart-rate variability, a recognized marker of a calmer nervous system (Wells and colleagues, 2002; Bowman and colleagues, 2015). One study put it plainly in its own title, finding that classical music "reduces environmental stress in kennelled dogs." When the same team compared genres across 38 dogs, the gentler styles drew the strongest relaxation response (Bowman and colleagues, 2017). So we keep the pace slow, the arrangement simple, the sound soft, and the music fresh.

A note from us: Calming music can help many dogs relax, but it is not a substitute for veterinary care. If your dog shows signs of severe anxiety, distress, or a medical problem, please talk with your veterinarian about a plan that fits your dog.
